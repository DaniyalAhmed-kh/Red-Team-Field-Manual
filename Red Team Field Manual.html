Preface

What this is

This is not a certification study guide. It is not a beginner tutorial. It is the operational reference that elite red teamers actually use — stripped of marketing fluff, written the way operators talk to each other. Every phase maps to real-world engagements. Every tool has been used in production. The A–Z guide at the back covers the concepts and mindset gaps that formal training programs never address.

Who built this framework: The techniques here come from APT-emulation work against Fortune 500 financial institutions, DoD contractor networks, critical infrastructure, and cloud-native environments. It reflects what works in 2025 against mature, instrumented, EDR-heavy targets — not lab machines.

How to use it: The 16 phases are sequential but non-linear. Real engagements loop. You'll be in lateral movement and suddenly need to revisit OSINT because you found a new subnet. Use this as a decision tree, not a checklist.

The number one mistake junior operators make is rushing to exploitation. Senior operators spend 40% of engagement time in recon and planning. The more you know before you touch a system, the quieter and more precise your attack will be. Quiet operations don't get caught. Noisy operations end with a phone call from the client's CISO stopping the engagement.

Planning & Scoping

The phase that separates professionals from amateurs. Poor scoping creates legal liability. Elite operators spend a fifth of the engagement here.

Legal Foundation

DocumentPurpose & What to Nail Down
Rules of EngagementIn-scope IP ranges, excluded systems (production DB, SCADA), time windows, emergency stop procedure, escalation contacts
Statement of WorkDeliverables, timeline, liability clause, payment schedule, NDA, breach notification responsibility
Get-Out-of-Jail LetterSigned authorization carried by every operator in the field. Physical copy. Two copies.
Data Handling PolicyHow captured PII, credentials, screenshots are stored, encrypted, and destroyed post-engagement
Deconfliction PlanSOC notification protocol so a real IR response doesn't get mixed up with your engagement traffic

Objective Definition

DecisionOptions & Operator Guidance
Engagement TypeBlack Box (no info, realistic), Grey Box (some info, efficient), Assumed Breach (post-compromise sim), Full Simulation (multi-month APT emulation)
Threat Actor ProfileAre you emulating APT29, FIN7, Scattered Spider? Research their TTPs and replicate them — this is adversary simulation, not generic pentesting
Crown JewelsDefine success before starting: DA cert, specific database, wire transfer system, classified document. Ambiguous objectives = watered-down reports
Success MetricsDwell time, number of detection gaps, objectives reached, mean-time-to-detect (MTTD), mean-time-to-respond (MTTR)

Infrastructure Setup

Your attack infrastructure is your OPSEC backbone. If it gets burned, your operation is over. Set this up weeks before the engagement starts.

ComponentSetup Notes
DomainsBuy 60+ days before engagement. Age them. Register in target's industry vertical for believability. Use separate registrar from personal domains.
RedirectorsVPS servers running Nginx/Apache with mod_rewrite. Forward valid C2 callbacks to C2 server. Forward all other traffic (Blue Team recon) to legit site.
C2 ServersNever expose directly. Sit behind redirectors. Different jurisdiction from redirectors. Wipe between engagements.
Operator OPSECDedicated VMs per campaign. No personal accounts anywhere. VPN to redirector before C2 access. Encrypted comms for team (Signal or Matrix).
LoggingLog every action with timestamps from day one. You will need this for the report. Use Ghostwriter or a simple timestamped markdown log.
The threat actor profile matters more than your tooling. If your client is a defense contractor, research what nation-state groups actually target defense contractors: their phishing lures, their C2 protocols, their preferred escalation paths. A red team that emulates APT41 accurately teaches the Blue Team what APT41 looks like on their wire. A generic pentest teaches them nothing about their real threat landscape.

Weaponization

Building the tools, payloads, and infrastructure for the engagement. Default tools get caught. Custom tooling is what gets you through mature EDR.

Payload Development

TechniqueWhat Elite Operators Do
Custom LoadersWrite shellcode loaders in Nim, Rust, or Go. New languages = no existing AV signatures. Encrypt shellcode (AES-256), decrypt at runtime.
Multi-StageStage 1 is tiny, checks environment (domain joined? VM? Right user?), only then fetches stage 2. Prevents detonation in sandbox.
Anti-SandboxSleep >120s (sandboxes fast-forward or timeout), check mouse movement, user activity, disk size >50GB, real domain check via LDAP.
String ObfuscationXOR all IOC strings at compile time. Decrypt at runtime. No static signatures on tool names, IPs, or registry keys.
BOF DevelopmentBeacon Object Files for Cobalt Strike/Havoc. Run in beacon memory, no new process. Harder to detect than fork-and-run.

Email gateway scans an HTML file with no attachment — user opens it in browser and the file downloads. Combine with an ISO or LNK container to bypass Mark-of-the-Web protections.

// Phase Four · TA0002
Execution

Running your code on target systems. The EDR problem is here. Every technique below is about executing while not spawning suspicious process trees.

IDTechniqueOperator Notes
T1059.001PowerShellPatch AMSI first. Use encoded commands. Never call powershell.exe from your implant directly — use a reflective loader instead.
T1218Signed Binary ProxyLOLBAS: rundll32, regsvr32, mshta, msiexec, certutil. All signed, all present by default. Each one has known detection and evasion patterns.
T1055Process InjectionInject into Explorer, Svchost, Teams. Classic injection, process hollowing, thread hijacking, APC injection. Use direct syscalls to avoid hooks.
T1055.004APC InjectionQueue APC to alertable thread. Less detectable than classic CreateRemoteThread injection in mature EDR environments.
The golden rule of execution: never create a new process from your implant parent. spawning cmd.exe, powershell.exe, or any new process from your beacon is detected by behavioral analytics in seconds. Use BOFs (run in beacon memory), or inject into already-running legitimate processes. SysWhispers3 for direct syscalls bypasses user-mode EDR hooks at the NTDLL layer.

Persistence

Surviving reboots, credential resets, and periodic security reviews. Choose methods that look like legitimate system behavior.

Windows Persistence

IDMethodDetection Risk
T1546.003WMI SubscriptionsPermanent WMI event subscriptions survive reboots. Very stealthy — no new services, no obvious registry keys. High-value targets only.
T1053.005Scheduled TasksName task like a legit Windows task. Set trigger to ONLOGON or a specific future time. Writable XML if you know the format.
T1547.001Run KeysHKCU works without admin. Noisier than WMI. Use when you need quick persistence and will clean up soon.
T1543.003Windows ServiceRequires admin. Name it close to existing services. Set description to match. Very persistent but logged in EventID 7045.

Linux & Cloud Persistence

MethodNotes
Cron JobsUser-level in crontab. Root-level in /etc/cron.d/. Name the file something boring. Run a callback every 15 minutes.
Systemd UnitCreate .service file. Set Restart=always. sudo systemctl enable —now. Clean, reliable, looks like legit software.
SSH KeysAdd your public key to ~/.ssh/authorized_keys. Root if you have it. Survives password changes. Simple and effective.
Cloud BackdoorsAzure: create service principal with Contributor. AWS: add IAM user with programmatic access. Invisible unless someone audits IAM.

// Phase Seven · TA0005
Defense Evasion

This isn't a phase — it's a discipline. Every action from initial access onward must be evaluated through an evasion lens. Stop thinking about it as a step.

IDTechniqueImplementation
T1562.006Patch ETW/AMSIPatch AmsiScanBuffer and EtwEventWrite in memory using direct syscalls. Do this at start of every PS session. One-liners widely available.
T1055.012Process HollowingSpawn legit process suspended, hollow memory, inject shellcode, resume. Harder to detect than external injection.
T1027ObfuscationXOR all strings at compile time. Encrypt config blobs. Stack strings. Use LLVM obfuscation passes. Randomize function names.
T1070Log TamperingSelectively delete specific Event IDs rather than clearing all logs — full clear is itself an alert. 4624, 4625, 4776 are the priority ones.
T1036MasqueradingRename tools to match legit processes. Copy file metadata and timestamps from the real binary. Place in legit-looking paths.
Know your target EDR before you run a single payload. CrowdStrike Falcon, SentinelOne, and Microsoft Defender for Endpoint each have distinct detection logic and different blind spots. Research the product. Find bypass blogs specific to that product version. Test in your lab against the same version they're running. A payload that bypasses Defender will get caught by CrowdStrike in two seconds.
Beacon sleep and jitter are underutilized. Most operators leave defaults (60 seconds, 0% jitter). Elite operators set 45-90 minute intervals with 30-50% jitter for long operations. A beacon that calls home once every hour during business hours generates almost no anomaly detection. Operate slow. Operate quiet. Operate patient.

Credential Access

Credentials are the master key in almost every environment. Get them and the network is yours. The techniques that work have barely changed — the detection has.

IDTechnique2025 Reality
T1003.001LSASS MemoryDirect LSASS dump is caught by every mature EDR. Use: Nanodump (custom minidump format), indirect dump via SSP, kernel-mode reader, or dump via comsvcs.dll through a LOLBin chain.
T1003.003NTDS.ditVSS shadow copy + copy NTDS.dit + SYSTEM hive. secretsdump.py extracts all domain hashes offline. Or DCSync if you have replication rights.
T1558.003KerberoastingRubeus kerberoast /outfile:hashes.txt then Hashcat -m 13100. Service accounts with weak passwords. Filter for adminCount=1 first.
T1550.002Pass-the-HashNTLM hash from LSASS → authenticate to other systems without cracking. CrackMapExec/NetExec for mass validation across the subnet.
T1555Password StoresChrome passwords via SharpChrome. Windows Credential Manager via SharpDPAPI. KeePass databases — find them on shares, keylog the master password.
DCSync is the cleanest DA-to-all-hashes path. Requires DS-Replication-Get-Changes and DS-Replication-Get-Changes-All. Once you have those rights (often via GenericAll on the domain), you never need to touch a DC disk. secretsdump.py -just-dc handles the rest. Golden Ticket (krbtgt hash) gives you 10-year domain persistence — survives password resets on every account except krbtgt itself.

Lateral Movement

Traversing the network with purpose. Every hop should be deliberate and planned. Noisy lateral movement triggers behavioral analytics almost immediately.

IDTechniqueDetection Profile
T1550.002Pass-the-HashMedium detection. Creates Type 3 logon events. Noisy across many hosts. Use targeted, not mass. Combine with NetExec for targeted validation.
T1021.006WinRMLow-medium detection. Evil-WinRM with stolen creds. Port 5985/5986. Creates WinRM logon events but less correlated than SMB.
T1021.003DCOMLow detection. MMC20.Application and ShellWindows DCOM objects for remote execution. Very few orgs monitor DCOM execution events.
T1021.002SMB Admin SharesHigh detection in mature envs. EventID 7045 (service install). Use only if necessary. wmiexec is better. Avoid PsExec in any mature SOC.
T1550.003Pass-the-TicketImport Kerberos TGT and authenticate as another user. No NTLM at all. Evades PtH detections. Needs Rubeus ptt command.
wmiexec.py is your daily driver for lateral movement when PsExec is too loud. It uses WMI, writes nothing to disk, and creates minimal log events. The syntax:

impacket-wmiexec DOMAIN/user:pass@target
For stealth over time, consider P2P beacons over SMB — spin up a beacon on the target that connects back to your already-implanted system rather than directly to C2. This keeps external callback count low and blends with normal SMB traffic.

The report is the product. The hack is just how you gather data for it. An operator who can't write is only half useful to a client.

Report Structure

SectionWhat Goes In It

Pre-Report Checklist

  • All operator timestamps logged and organized by action
  • Every persistence mechanism cleaned up and documented
  • All created accounts removed and listed in appendix
  • Credentials captured listed (hashed, not plaintext in report)
  • Attack path diagram built in draw.io or equivalent
  • MITRE ATT&CK Navigator heatmap exported
  • Every finding has at least one screenshot as evidence
  • Business impact written for non-technical audience
  • Remediation steps reviewed for accuracy by technical operator
  • Executive summary reviewed by someone who isn't technical
  • All captured sensitive data (PII, creds) destroyed per policy
  • Debrief scheduled within 5 business days of report delivery

A to Z

The Operator's Encyclopedia

The concepts, mindsets, techniques, and hard-won lessons that don't fit neatly into a phase checklist. Read these when you're stuck, when you're bored, or when you want to think like a real operator instead of following a recipe.

A
Active Directory
The Domain You Actually Own
AD is present in nearly every enterprise and it is almost always misconfigured. Not slightly — embarrassingly. The average domain has Kerberoastable service accounts with weak passwords, ADCS misconfigs that allow any user to get a DA certificate, excessive ACL permissions granted years ago and never reviewed, and at least one account with DCSync rights that nobody remembers adding.

Your first hour in a domain should always go the same way: run SharpHound, import to BloodHound, run the four attack path queries, check Certipy for ADCS issues, and look at what groups your current user belongs to. You will find a path. Always.
BloodHoundSharpHoundCertipyPowerViewRubeus
In 12 years of red teaming, I have never failed to get Domain Admin in an Active Directory environment. Not once. The only variable is how long it takes. — Senior RT Operator
B
BloodHound
God Mode for Active Directory
BloodHound changed red teaming permanently when it was released. Before it, operators spent days manually enumerating AD relationships. Now SharpHound collects in minutes and the graph answers questions that would have taken a week to answer manually.

The attack paths that consistently produce results: "Shortest Path to Domain Admins", "Find AS-REP Roastable Users", "Find Kerberoastable High Value Targets", "Users with Foreign Domain Group Membership", and "Shortest Paths to Unconstrained Delegation Systems." Run all of them. Every time. The Community Edition (CE) is now the maintained fork — use that, not the old version.

C2 Profile Design
The Art of the Invisible Channel
Default C2 frameworks are caught immediately by mature SOCs. A default Cobalt Strike installation has been fingerprinted so thoroughly that Defenders have Sigma rules, Suricata rules, and YARA rules specifically for it.

What makes a good C2 profile: It mimics a real application's traffic patterns exactly. Not just the headers — the timing, the packet sizes, the response structure, the error behavior. Mimic Microsoft Graph API, Slack, Dropbox, or another SaaS your target uses daily. The Blue Team can't block it without blocking the real service. Customize every HTTP header, the User-Agent, the URI paths, the response content. Make the server return real-looking JSON even if it's garbage to the human eye.
Cobalt Strike + Malleable C2HavocSliverC2concealer
A good C2 profile took you 6 hours to build. That 6 hours buys you weeks of undetected operation. Budget for it.
D
DCSync
Domain Compromise Without Touching a DC
DCSync is the technique where you impersonate a domain controller's replication process to pull password hashes from the real DC. You need DS-Replication-Get-Changes and DS-Replication-Get-Changes-All rights — which are granted to Domain Admins by default, but are also sometimes granted to other accounts or groups by overzealous admins.

The workflow: Find an account with DCSync rights via BloodHound → escalate to that account → run secretsdump.py or Mimikatz lsadump::dcsync → extract krbtgt hash → forge Golden Ticket → game over. The krbtgt hash gives you 10-year Kerberos ticket validity. Even if every password in the domain gets reset, your Golden Ticket still works until krbtgt is rotated twice.
secretsdump.pyMimikatz (lsadump::dcsync)Rubeus (Golden Ticket)
DCSync is so clean it should be illegal. It leaves almost no trace, requires no disk access on the DC, and gives you everything.
E
EDR Bypass Mindset
Don't Fight It — Route Around It
Most junior operators treat EDR bypass as a puzzle to solve once. Senior operators understand it's a continuous arms race and approach it as a research problem, not a tooling problem.

The methodology that works: Identify the exact EDR product and version. Set up a lab VM with the same product. Understand how that specific EDR instruments the OS — which kernel callbacks it registers, which API calls it hooks, which ETW providers it consumes. Then find the gaps in that specific instrumentation. CrowdStrike has different blind spots than SentinelOne. Understanding the product matters more than having the right tool.
SysWhispers3ThreatCheckScarecrowFreezePPLdump
The operator who understands how EDR works wins. The one who just runs tools loses eventually.
F
Fileless Operation
Living Entirely in Memory
Fileless doesn't mean no files ever touch disk — it means your primary payload execution chain avoids writing to disk. Reflective DLL injection, process hollowing, PowerShell entirely in memory, Beacon Object Files running inside an existing process — all of these leave minimal or no disk artifacts.

The principle: Your implant should live in the memory of a legitimate process. Your tools should execute inside that implant's memory space. Data should be processed in memory and streamed out. Avoid staging files on disk. If you must write to disk, write to a path that isn't monitored (%APPDATA%, temp folders), use timestomping, and clean up immediately.
BOF FrameworkDonutInject-AssemblyPowerShell (AMSI bypassed)
The best artifact is no artifact. The best evidence is no evidence. Operate in memory wherever possible.
G
Golden Ticket
The Skeleton Key That Never Expires
A Golden Ticket is a forged Kerberos Ticket Granting Ticket (TGT) signed with the domain's krbtgt account hash. Because Kerberos validates tickets using that hash, and because you control the hash, you can forge a ticket for any user, any group membership, and any expiry time you want — up to 10 years.

What makes it powerful: Password resets for regular accounts don't invalidate a Golden Ticket. The only remediation is rotating krbtgt twice with a gap in between — which most orgs do not know how to do correctly, and which causes significant disruption. This is why you document access, don't leave Golden Tickets running in production engagements, and clean up after yourself.
Mimikatz (kerberos::golden)Rubeus (golden)Impacket (ticketer.py)
Getting the krbtgt hash is the end of the engagement. Everything after that is cleanup and documentation.
H
HTML Smuggling
The Technique That Broke Email Gateways
HTML Smuggling builds the malicious payload client-side inside a browser using JavaScript. The email gateway sees an HTML attachment containing JavaScript — no embedded file, no obvious payload. The browser executes the JS, assembles a Blob(), and triggers a download of the assembled file directly from memory.

Why it still works in 2025: Most email gateways can't execute JavaScript in sandboxes at scale. The payload is assembled after delivery. Combine with an ISO container (bypasses MOTW on unpatched systems) or a LNK file pointing to a PowerShell loader for a complete, gate-bypassing delivery chain. Defender SmartScreen increasingly flags LNKs — test your specific target's config.
Custom HTML templatesDemiguiseGadgetToJScript
The day this stops working, something else will take its place. The delivery mechanism changes; the principle — reassemble on the client side — stays the same.
I
Implant Architecture
Design Your Agent Like You Design Software
Most operators grab an off-the-shelf agent and use it as-is. Elite operators think about their implant the way a software engineer thinks about a production system: what happens when it crashes? What happens if C2 goes down? How does it reconnect? How does it fail safely?

Design considerations for mature engagements: Primary + fallback C2 channels. Sleep with jitter. Anti-sandbox checks before any callback. Environment validation (domain joined? specific user logged in?). Automatic self-deletion after X days of no contact. These aren't luxuries — they're the difference between a one-week engagement and a three-month simulation.
Cobalt Strike (BOFs)Havoc (Demon)Sliver (multiplex)Custom Nim/Rust loaders
Your implant is your presence in the environment. A flaky implant means a flaky operation.
J
Jump Hosts & Pivoting
Hopping Through Segmented Networks
Network segmentation is the defense. Pivoting is the offense. Every time you move through a compromised system to reach the next network segment, you're pivoting. Done well, your C2 traffic never crosses the firewall directly — it flows through systems already inside the perimeter.

Pivot techniques that work: SOCKS proxy through your beacon (Cobalt Strike socks5, Sliver socks5 server), SSH tunneling via compromised Linux hosts, Ligolo-ng for full layer-3 tunneling through implants. For isolated segments with no internet access at all: P2P beacons over SMB that daisy-chain back to an internet-connected host. Draw your network topology as you discover it — pivoting without a map gets messy fast.
Ligolo-ngChiselCS SOCKS ProxySSH tunnelsfrp
Segmentation only stops operators who don't know how to pivot. Learn to pivot and segmentation becomes a speed bump.
K
Kerberoasting
The Gift That Keeps Giving
Kerberoasting has been a known attack since 2014. It still works in 2025 because organizations still run service accounts with weak passwords and high privileges. Any domain user can request a service ticket for any SPN. That ticket is encrypted with the service account's password hash. Take it offline and crack it.

Prioritization matters: Don't Kerberoast everything and hope. Filter for service accounts that are members of privileged groups (adminCount=1, Domain Admins, Server Operators). Those are the ones worth cracking. Use hashcat with a targeted wordlist: rockyou.txt + company-specific words + common service account patterns (Service2023!, CompanyName123). Service accounts often haven't had their password changed in years.
Rubeus (kerberoast)GetUserSPNs.pyHashcat (-m 13100)BloodHound (find targets)
Every single domain has Kerberoastable accounts. The question is whether any of them have weak passwords and high privilege. Usually yes.
L
LSASS Without Getting Caught
The Goldmine and the Minefield
LSASS contains the credentials for every user logged into the system. It's also the most monitored process in Windows. Direct dumping with ProcDump, Task Manager, or even comsvcs.dll triggers immediate alerts in any EDR worth its license fee.

What actually works in 2025: Nanodump creates a custom minidump format that doesn't match known signatures. Indirect LSASS dumping via a custom NTDLL handle that doesn't go through the hooked APIs. SSP injection registers a custom Security Support Provider that receives credentials as they're used. Kernel-mode dumpers that read LSASS from kernel space, bypassing all user-mode hooks. Each of these requires a different level of sophistication — pick the one that matches your target's security maturity.
NanodumpHandleKatzPPLBladeMimikatz (offline)
Direct LSASS dump is a rookie move in 2025. If your EDR doesn't catch it, your target doesn't have a real EDR.
M
MFA — It's Not a Hard Stop
Every MFA Implementation Has a Gap
Multi-factor authentication stops password stuffing. It does not stop a determined operator. Every MFA implementation has attack surfaces: push notification fatigue (bomb the user until they approve), adversary-in-the-middle proxy that captures the session token after MFA (Evilginx), voice phishing to the helpdesk, SIM swapping for SMS-based MFA, or legacy authentication protocols that bypass MFA entirely (NTLM, basic auth on old endpoints).

The MFA fatigue workflow that consistently works: Spray until you have valid credentials. Start sending push notifications repeatedly — 20 to 30 in a row. Then call the target impersonating IT, tell them there's been unusual activity and you need them to approve one more notification to confirm their identity. Approval rate in tested environments: around 60%.
Evilginx 3MFASweepo365sprayAADInternals
When a client says "we have MFA on everything so we're fine" — that's when you start looking for legacy auth endpoints.
N
NTLM Relay
The Protocol Vulnerability That Never Dies
NTLM relay attacks have existed for over two decades. They still work because NTLM is still used everywhere — on internal network paths, printer protocols, file server communication, and SMB shares. When a system tries to authenticate to your controlled listener using NTLM, you can relay that authentication to a real service and authenticate as that user.

The modern attack chain: Responder to capture NTLM challenges on the LAN → ntlmrelayx.py to relay to targets without SMB signing → dump SAM database or get a shell. With MS-EFSRPC or PrinterBug you can coerce authentication from specific machines including Domain Controllers. Relay a DC's machine account to another DC over LDAP → add yourself to Domain Admins. This chain compromises fully patched environments with no CVE exploitation needed.
Responderntlmrelayx.pyPetitPotamPrinterBugmitm6
NTLM relay is the attack that never ages. As long as SMB signing isn't enforced network-wide, it works.
O
OPSEC
The Discipline That Keeps You Employed
Operational Security isn't a checklist item — it's a mindset that governs every decision during an engagement. Your personal IP address should never appear in target logs. Your personal identity should never be associated with attacker infrastructure. Your real C2 server should never be reachable from the open internet without going through redirectors first.

OPSEC failures that ended real engagements: Using a personal email to register a domain. Letting your home IP slip through a VPN gap into target logs. Using the same tool binary across multiple engagements (same file hash). Running recon from the same IP you use for C2. Posting about the engagement on social media. Forgetting to clean up your operator notes in a file you staged on target. Think like someone will forensically analyze everything you touch. Because if the engagement goes wrong, they will.
Dedicated VMsMullvad VPNSignalProtonmailSeparate hardware
OPSEC failures don't just burn the engagement. They can burn your reputation, your company, and in some cases your freedom.
P
Phishing in 2025
Still Number One. Always Number One.
People keep waiting for phishing to stop working. It won't. The reason it stays at the top of initial access statistics year after year is that it targets the hardest problem in security: human judgment under time pressure. Training helps at the margins. It never eliminates the attack surface.

What's working right now: Business email compromise with high-context lures (reference real projects from LinkedIn), QR code phishing that bypasses URL scanning (phone cameras open the URL in a mobile browser with different security posture), HTML smuggling with DocuSign/SharePoint lures, and callback phishing (the target calls a fake IT number and gets socially engineered over the phone). The highest-performing campaigns have one thing in common: they look exactly like something the target was already expecting to receive.
Evilginx 3GoPhishKing PhisherModlishkaEvilnoVNC
The best phish is one the target wanted to receive. Do your OSINT. Write a lure that makes sense for that specific person.
Q
Quick Wins
What to Look for in the First 30 Minutes
When you get your first foothold, there's a sequence of fast checks that frequently produce immediate escalation without any exploitation at all. Run these before anything else:

The 30-minute checklist: (1) What groups is this user in? Check for unexpected privileged group membership. (2) Are there any GPP passwords in SYSVOL? (3) Is AlwaysInstallElevated enabled? (4) Any writable service binary paths? (5) What network shares are accessible — run Snaffler. (6) Is SMB signing disabled network-wide? (7) Are there any ADCS templates with ESC1 misconfigs? (8) Which users have Kerberos pre-auth disabled? Most of these checks take under 5 minutes and frequently produce immediate domain compromise.
WinPEASPowerUpSnafflerCertipyRubeus
Slow down and run quick checks first. The most embarrassing path to DA is the one that was sitting there in the first 10 minutes and you missed it because you rushed.
R
Redirectors
The Infrastructure That Keeps You Alive
A redirector is a VPS server that sits between the target and your C2. When the Blue Team inevitably investigates your C2 traffic, they find the redirector IP — not your C2 server. The redirector forwards valid C2 callbacks to the real C2 and redirects everything else (sandbox analysis, SOC investigation) to an innocent website.

The correct setup: Redirector runs Nginx with a specific Allowlist rule — only requests with your exact C2 profile URI and User-Agent get proxied to C2. Everything else gets a 301 to a real website. The C2 server firewall only accepts connections from the redirector IPs. The C2 server has no domain name. If the redirector gets burned, you spin up a new one and the C2 server lives on. Build at least two redirectors per engagement — one HTTPS, one DNS.
Nginx (mod_rewrite)ApachesocatDigitalOcean/Vultr VPSCloudflare Workers
Your C2 IP getting burned ends the engagement. Your redirector IP getting burned costs you $6 for a new VPS. Always use redirectors.
S
Syscalls
Bypassing EDR at the Lowest Layer
EDR products instrument Windows by hooking NTDLL functions in user-mode. When your code calls NtAllocateVirtualMemory, the EDR intercepts that call, inspects it, decides if it's malicious, then (usually) passes it to the kernel. Hooking is how EDR sees what you're doing.

Direct syscalls bypass this entirely. Instead of calling through NTDLL (where hooks live), you call the kernel directly using the raw syscall instruction with the correct System Service Number (SSN). SysWhispers3 generates the stubs automatically. No NTDLL, no hooks, no EDR visibility. The counter to this is kernel-mode EDR with PatchGuard-bypass capabilities — which exist, but are rarer. Indirect syscalls (call through legitimate NTDLL code that eventually hits the syscall) are the next layer when direct syscalls get caught.
SysWhispers3HellsGateHalosGateRecycledGate
If you don't understand how NTDLL hooking works, you can't reliably bypass it. Read the internals before you use the tools.
T
Threat Intelligence
Know Who Your Client Actually Faces
Red teaming without threat intelligence is just pentesting with a fancier name. The entire value proposition of adversary emulation is that you replicate the TTPs of the actual threat actors targeting your client's industry. That requires research.

The pre-engagement research workflow: Look up the client's industry sector in MITRE ATT&CK Groups. Identify 2-3 active threat actor groups targeting that sector. Pull their MITRE ATT&CK profiles. Find their recent campaign reports (Mandiant, CrowdStrike, SentinelOne, industry ISACs). Build a list of their preferred initial access methods, persistence techniques, C2 protocols, and target data types. Then structure your engagement to use those same TTPs. Now your report tells the client exactly whether they would have caught APT29 — not some hypothetical generic attacker.
MITRE ATT&CKOpenCTIMISPMandiant ReportsCISA Advisories
The question your report should answer isn't "can we be hacked." It's "would we survive APT29 targeting us right now." Answer that one.
U
Unconstrained Delegation
The Most Dangerous Misconfiguration in AD
Unconstrained delegation means: any user who authenticates to this machine will have their TGT (Ticket Granting Ticket) stored in memory on that machine. Including Domain Controllers when they authenticate to printers, file servers, or any other service set to unconstrained.

The attack chain: Find a system with unconstrained delegation (common on old servers, printers, legacy application hosts). Get admin on it. Wait for a Domain Controller to authenticate to it, or coerce authentication using PrinterBug/PetitPotam. The DC's TGT lands in memory. Extract it with Rubeus. Use it to authenticate as the DC. DCSync the entire domain. This technique works on fully patched, fully updated environments — it's a configuration issue, not a software vulnerability.
BloodHound (find targets)Rubeus (monitor+extract)SpoolSample (PrinterBug)PetitPotam
Find unconstrained delegation systems on day one. They're frequently your fastest path to DA without touching any CVE.
V
Vulnerability Chaining
Three Low Findings Equal One Critical
Standalone vulnerabilities often look manageable. A low CVSS finding here, a medium there. What pentesters miss and red teamers demonstrate is that these findings chain together into catastrophic attack paths that no individual finding's severity reflects.

A real chain from a 2024 engagement: Exposed Jenkins with guest access (Medium) → read build configuration files that contained plaintext credentials for a service account (Medium) → service account had GenericWrite on a high-privilege group in AD (Medium) → added operator-controlled account to Domain Admins (Critical impact). Every finding on its own was "patch it sometime." Together they were a direct path to full domain compromise from the internet. Your report must show these chains, not just list individual findings.
BloodHound (chain visualization)Draw.io (attack paths)ATT&CK Navigator
The most important sentence in your report is the one that connects finding #2 to finding #7 and shows how they produced a full compromise together.
W
WMI — Windows Management Instrumentation
The Stealthiest Execution Primitive in Windows
WMI is a legitimate Windows management framework. Every IT department uses it. Defenders can't just block it. And it provides nearly everything an attacker needs: remote code execution, process enumeration, system information, event subscriptions for persistence, and lateral movement — all through a legitimate, signed, always-present Windows component.

Why wmiexec is better than PsExec for lateral movement: No new service installed. No binary dropped to disk (in most implementations). Uses port 135/DCOM, which is almost always open internally. Generates far fewer suspicious events. The downside: slower than PsExec, output retrieval requires a writable share. For stealth over speed, WMI wins every time.
wmiexec.pyInvoke-WMIMethod (PS)SharpWMIWMIImplant
WMI is in every Windows environment, can't be removed, and is used by every IT team. It's the perfect attacker tool hiding in plain sight.
X
XXE & SSRF
Web App Vulns That Reach the Internal Network
XXE (XML External Entity) and SSRF (Server-Side Request Forgery) are particularly valuable to red teamers because they don't just compromise the web application — they open a tunnel into the internal network. The server makes requests on your behalf to internal systems, cloud metadata endpoints, and internal APIs that your external position could never reach directly.

The SSRF-to-full-compromise chain: Find SSRF in a public web app → point it at the AWS metadata endpoint (169.254.169.254) → retrieve IAM credentials → use those credentials with aws-cli → enumerate S3 buckets, EC2 instances, Lambda functions → escalate via IAM privilege escalation → full cloud account compromise. This chain doesn't touch a single endpoint or involve any malware. Pure web app exploitation into cloud environment.
Burp Suite ProSSRFmapGopherusaws-cli (post-exploit)
SSRF to cloud metadata is one of the cleanest paths from web app to cloud admin. Look for it on every external assessment.
Y
Your Report Changes Things
Or It Doesn't. Your Choice.
Most penetration test reports sit in a shared drive, get reviewed once, have a few tickets created, and are forgotten within six months. The organization is barely more secure than before the engagement. This happens because operators write reports for other operators, not for the people who make security investment decisions.

The report that changes things: Speaks business risk at the top. "We accessed the database containing all customer financial records, which represents regulatory exposure of approximately $X million under GDPR Article 83." Gives non-technical readers a clear picture of what happened without jargon. Shows the remediation path with specific steps and effort estimates. Then goes deep technical in the appendices for the engineering team. Two audiences, two levels of document, one cohesive report. That's the one that gets budgets approved and controls implemented.
GhostwriterPwndocDradisPlexTracVectr
A great hack with a mediocre report produces no lasting change. A mediocre hack with a great report moves the security needle. Write the report.
Z
Zero Day Mindset
You Don't Need Zero Days. You Need to Think Like a Researcher.
Junior operators think elite hackers use zero days. Most elite red team engagements are won with techniques that are years old, against misconfigurations that have existed since the domain was built. The zero day mindset isn't about finding new CVEs — it's about looking at every system with the curiosity of a researcher and asking: "what assumption did the person who configured this make, and how is that assumption wrong?"

The researcher's approach to every target: What does this component trust? What data does it process without validation? What identity does it run as? What happens if I give it input it wasn't designed for? What implicit trust relationships exist between this and other systems? Apply these questions to everything — AD, web apps, cloud configs, network architecture. 99% of real-world compromises don't need a zero day. They need an operator who thinks carefully about trust, assumptions, and design failures.
